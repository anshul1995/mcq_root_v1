All of them have the following features, which you should keep in mind while creating your MCQ:

  1. The question should be very clear, and not likely to be ambiguous.
  2. There must be only one correct answer.
  3. All 3 incorrect options should be plausible. If an option is obviously wrong for all the students, then it is not a good option. A good incorrect option should be plausible to a student who has not yet learned the skill or knowledge that the MCQ is trying to assess.
  4. Your MCQ should require the students to think more than simply repeating memorized facts. Higher-order thinking requires students to do something with the facts: apply them, analyze them, organize them, align them to concepts and connect to other concepts, question and critique them, and use them to creatively solve problems.
